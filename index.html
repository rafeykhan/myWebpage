Collaborative Pushing

Objective: program a set of two robots to perform a collaborative box pushing task. Goal is to find a long box and then to move this box through a previously specified exit.

Master-follower combination was implemented. The master would use the sonars to detect the box and allign itself with it. Master would then send coordinates over bluetooth to the follower and wait for it to return 'ready' message once it is in place.

IEEE Robotics Competition 2017 (Denver,CO)

Competition date: April 1st 2017.

Motivation:
The field is meant to simulate tunnels containing infrastructure such as steel cables, underground rails and electrical wires. Surface obstacles and anomalies represent typical operational challenge.

Objectives:
Map the field and its tunnel while avoiding obstacles
open the lid on the container to reveal the dice
read and display the number on the die.

The robot is composed of major sub-system layers:

Perception: IR sensors and Vision to detect obstacles and read the dice number. LIDAR was employed initially to detect obstacles, but due to restrictions in weight and size, IR sensors were employed instead.
Computer vision was used to read the dice. I used OpenCV and image processing to determine the number of dots on the die and display it.
To sense underground wires, I built an EMF sensor using an Arduino which worked extremely well.

Arm: A linear actuator was used to grab and lift the lid.

Drive and Navigation: Decision-making and path planning algorithms were written for the robot to function autonomously, depending on the data from the camera and sensors.

Technologies used:
Python, C
Computer Vision, OpenCV, ROS
CAD, 3D fabrication
LIDAR, IR, camera

Result:
Placed 5th out of 32 teams across the nation.

Team:
De Do, Jarrod Nix, Jake Skodak, Greg Stout, Abdul R. Khan

I really enjoyed my time working on this robot. I gained lots of valuable experience and learned from the mistakes and issues that I came across. But hey, I won a cash award though!

Image Processing

Template Matching using Normalized Convolution

The fundamental method of calculating the image correlation is called cross-correlation, which essentially is a simple sum of pairwise multiplications of corresponding pixel values of the image and template. We also subtract the mean image brightness from each pixel value to make the results invariant to brightness change. Finally, we normalize the result to be between 0 and 255 and store the output to the array. Highest score coincides with the best match.
